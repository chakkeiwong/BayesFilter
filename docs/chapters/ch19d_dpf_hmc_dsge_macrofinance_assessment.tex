\chapter{Learned Transport Operators and Implementation Mathematics}
\label{ch:bf-dpf-learned-ot}

The previous chapter developed differentiable resampling up to the level of
entropic optimal transport and barycentric projection.  This chapter studies a
further approximation layer: learned or amortized transport operators that are
trained to imitate a previously defined transport map.  The central mathematical
point is simple but essential: a learned operator is not just a faster solver.
It is a new approximation to an already relaxed target construction.

This distinction matters because the DPF literature often presents neural
transport acceleration in computational terms, while the monograph must treat it
in target-status terms.  If the exact resampling map is replaced by an OT
relaxation, and the OT relaxation is then replaced by a learned operator,
BayesFilter must keep track of both approximation layers.

The source spine for this chapter is deliberately narrower than a generic neural
transport survey.  The OT teacher map comes from the entropy-regularized
particle-filter construction of
\citet{corenflos2021differentiable}; the computational motivation for
amortizing Sinkhorn comes from the Sinkhorn cost structure of
\citet{cuturi2013sinkhorn} and \citet{peyre2019computational}; and the
architecture discussion uses permutation-equivariant set operators as the
relevant design family \citep{zaheer2017deep,lee2019set}.  These sources do not
prove that a learned operator preserves the posterior target.  They identify the
map family being approximated and the architectural constraints that any such
approximation must respect.

\section{The hierarchy of approximations}
\label{sec:bf-learned-ot-hierarchy}

The resampling constructions of the rebuilt DPF block now form a clear
hierarchy:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item standard categorical resampling;
  \item transport-based or entropic-OT relaxation of categorical resampling;
  \item learned or amortized approximation to the OT map.
\end{enumerate}
At each step in this hierarchy, one moves farther from the exact categorical map
and gains a different form of smoothness or speed.  The learned-OT question is
therefore not only whether a neural operator is fast or differentiable, but
which map it is approximating and what error remains after that approximation.

\section{Teacher map and learned map}
\label{sec:bf-learned-ot-teacher}

Let $T_\varepsilon$ denote the entropically regularized OT barycentric map from
the previous chapter.  Given a weighted particle cloud
$\{(x_i,w_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, the transport-relaxed equal-weight cloud is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-learned-ot-teacher-map}
  \tilde X_{\mathrm{OT}} = T_\varepsilon(X,w),
\end{equation}
where $X=(x_1,\ldots,x_N)$ collects the particle locations and $w$ the weight
vector.

A learned or amortized operator introduces a parametric approximation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-learned-ot-student-map}
  \tilde X_{\psi} = T_\psi(X,w,\varepsilon),
\end{equation}
with trainable parameters $\psi$.  The training objective is typically to make
$T_\psi$ imitate $T_\varepsilon$ on a chosen distribution of particle clouds.
Thus the teacher-student relationship is explicit:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-learned-ot-training-objective}
  \psi^* \in \arg\min_\psi
  \mathbb E_{(X,w) \sim \mathcal D}
  \bigl[ \mathcal L\bigl(T_\psi(X,w,\varepsilon),
  T_\varepsilon(X,w)\bigr) \bigr],
\end{equation}
for some training distribution $\mathcal D$ and some discrepancy functional
$\mathcal L$.

The mathematical significance of
\eqref{eq:bf-learned-ot-training-objective} is that it defines learned OT as an
approximation to a teacher map, not as a direct theorem about the original
categorical resampling law.  The learned map inherits the OT interpretation only
through the quality of that approximation.

This also fixes the role of neural architecture.  If $T_\psi$ acts on an
unordered particle cloud, then the natural invariance requirement is permutation
equivariance:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-learned-ot-equivariance}
  T_\psi(PX,Pw,\varepsilon) = P\,T_\psi(X,w,\varepsilon)
\end{equation}
for any particle permutation matrix $P$.  DeepSets-style shared row maps and
global pooling \citep{zaheer2017deep}, or attention-based set operators
\citep{lee2019set}, are relevant because they make this symmetry a design
constraint rather than a hope.  Symmetry alone is not a target guarantee, but
violating it creates an immediate implementation bug: the learned resampling
measure would depend on arbitrary input ordering.

\section{Approximation residuals and target shift}
\label{sec:bf-learned-ot-residuals}

Let
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-learned-ot-residual}
  R_\psi(X,w,\varepsilon)
  = T_\psi(X,w,\varepsilon) - T_\varepsilon(X,w)
\end{equation}
define the map-level approximation residual.  This residual is not yet the same
thing as posterior error, because the learned operator acts only on one layer of
the filtering recursion.  Nevertheless, it is mathematically important because
it tells us how far the learned resampling map is from the OT baseline that the
previous chapter already identified as a relaxed transport construction.

The key interpretive rule is therefore:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the OT baseline already differs from exact categorical resampling;
  \item the learned map differs from that OT baseline by the residual
    \eqref{eq:bf-learned-ot-residual};
  \item any later posterior discrepancy may reflect both layers.
\end{itemize}
Thus a reported posterior shift under learned OT should not be described only as
"neural approximation error" in a vague sense.  It is better understood as the
composition of:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item the OT relaxation relative to the exact resampling mechanism;
  \item the learned-map residual relative to the OT baseline.
\end{enumerate}
This layered interpretation is essential for later HMC analysis.
This also implies an important guardrail for later chapters and
experiments: a map-level residual or transport-cost discrepancy does not by
itself prove a specific posterior error size.  To make a stronger posterior
claim one still needs additional evidence, for example posterior comparisons
against the teacher OT baseline or against a trusted reference target.

\section{Training-distribution dependence}
\label{sec:bf-learned-ot-training-distribution}

A learned transport operator is always trained relative to a specific family of
particle clouds, weights, dimensions, and regularization parameters.  Let
$\mathcal D$ denote this training distribution.  The approximation guarantee one
might hope for is therefore local to the support of $\mathcal D$, not global in
all state-space models.  In particular, a small residual on the training family
does not by itself imply a small target shift under all out-of-distribution
weight geometries or state-space models.

This dependence can enter through several channels:
\begin{itemize}
  \item state dimension;
  \item number of particles;
  \item geometry of the weight simplex;
  \item regularization parameter $\varepsilon$;
  \item cloud geometry induced by the underlying state-space model.
\end{itemize}
If a learned operator is trained on low-dimensional clouds or a narrow range of
regularization strengths, its use outside that regime becomes an extrapolation
problem rather than a direct continuation of the training objective.

This is why the advanced student comparison source is helpful: it states
explicitly that the learned OT operator there is trained on a restricted cloud
family and a restricted regularization range, and it records a nonzero
approximation residual together with a posterior-level shift.  BayesFilter
should preserve that kind of honesty in the monograph.  The correct mathematical
reading is not that the learned operator "works in general," but that it works
relative to the teacher map on the training family to the recorded level of
approximation.

\section{Implementation-facing mathematics}
\label{sec:bf-learned-ot-implementation-math}

The implementation consequences of learned OT are not purely engineering
convenience.  They follow from the mathematics of what is being approximated.

\subsection{Runtime interpretation}

If $T_\psi$ replaces the iterative Sinkhorn solve by a single forward pass, then
a substantial runtime reduction is possible.  That reduction is real, but it
must be interpreted correctly: the runtime is reduced because one is evaluating a
trained approximation to the OT map, not because the exact OT computation has
somehow become free.

\subsection{Deterministic evaluation}

A learned operator is typically deterministic conditional on its inputs and its
parameters.  This can simplify later gradient propagation and repeated HMC
likelihood evaluations compared with stochastic inner resampling schemes.  But
this determinism does not eliminate the target issue; it only changes the nature
of the approximation.

\subsection{State-dimension and architecture dependence}

A learned set-based operator may be intrinsically tied to a fixed state
dimension, particle count regime, or attention architecture.  Those are not
merely software choices; they define the class of maps the learned operator can
represent.  Hence the mathematical object being approximated depends partly on
the architecture family itself.

\section{Why this chapter stops short of an HMC verdict}
\label{sec:bf-learned-ot-no-hmc-verdict}

The chapter has now identified learned or amortized OT as a second approximation
layer above the OT baseline.  That is enough to prepare the later HMC chapter,
but not enough to settle the HMC question.  The unresolved issue is no longer
how the learned map is trained, but what posterior object HMC would target if it
used the learned operator inside the likelihood path.

That question belongs to the later HMC-suitability chapter because it requires a
joint analysis of:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the baseline particle-filter target;
  \item the particle-flow proposal layer;
  \item the OT relaxation layer;
  \item the learned-operator approximation layer;
  \item and the value-gradient path seen by HMC.
\end{itemize}

\section{What this chapter does and does not claim}
\label{sec:bf-learned-ot-boundary}

This chapter develops the teacher-versus-learned-map distinction for OT-based
resampling approximations.  It does \emph{not} yet claim:
\begin{itemize}
  \item that learned OT is equivalent to the OT baseline;
  \item that the OT baseline is equivalent to exact categorical resampling;
  \item that a small map-level residual implies negligible posterior shift in all
    models;
  \item that learned OT is already justified as a final HMC target for nonlinear
    DSGE or MacroFinance models.
\end{itemize}
Its role is narrower and mathematically prior: it identifies learned transport
operators as approximations to an already relaxed transport baseline and states
why that layered approximation must remain explicit in the monograph.
